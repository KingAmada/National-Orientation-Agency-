Separation of Powers

At federal and state levels: Executive (President/Governor), Legislature (National/State Assembly), Judiciary (Supreme/High Courts). The 1999 Constitution is supreme; any conflicting law is void. No major amendments to structure in 2025.

Fundamental Rights (Chapter IV, 1999 Constitution - Full Provisions)

Chapter IV (Sections 33-46) guarantees these enforceable rights to ALL persons in Nigeria, including foreigners. Derived from official text; no 2025 amendments.

Section Right & Description
33: Right to Life Every person has right to life; no deprivation except by court sentence for capital offense. Exceptions: self-defense, arrest, suppressing riot/insurrection.
34: Dignity of Human Person Protected from torture, inhuman/degrading treatment, slavery, forced labor. No slavery/servitude except as court punishment.
35: Personal Liberty No arbitrary arrest/detention. Informed of reasons in understandable language; brought to court within reasonable time (24h urban, 48h rural, 96h max w/o court order). Bail for non-capital offenses.
36: Fair Hearing Presumed innocent; fair/public hearing by independent court within reasonable time. Right to lawyer, interpreter, public trial (except security reasons), no retroactive criminal laws.
37: Private & Family Life Privacy of home, correspondence, telephone protected; no unlawful search/seizure without warrant.
38: Thought, Conscience, Religion Freedom to change/practice religion alone/with others; includes propagation. No forced membership.
39: Expression & Press Freedom to receive/impart ideas/information without interference. Own/maintain media; no censorship except wartime.
40: Assembly & Association Peaceful assembly/association; form/trade unions, register societies.
41: Movement Free movement/residence in Nigeria; exit/re-enter. Restrictions only by law (e.g., security, public health).
42: Freedom from Discrimination No discrimination in citizenship, rights based on community, ethnic group, place of origin, sex, religion, politics.
43-44: Property Acquire/own immovable property anywhere; no compulsory acquisition without compensation/law.
45: Derogations Rights derogable in emergencies (e.g., war) but only by law, not absolutely (e.g., no derogation from life/dignity).
46: Enforcement Apply to High Court for redress; court can issue orders, compensation. No ouster clauses.

Limitations

Rights not absolute; balanced with public interest (e.g., movement subject to immigration laws). Derogations in states of emergency.

Enforcement (2025)

Contact Embassy/Consulate first. Sue in State/Federal High Court (no fees for rights suits). Fundamental Rights (Enforcement Procedure) Rules 2009 guide process.

Source: Official 1999 Constitution (as amended); full text at nigerian-constitution.com.

Legal System & Enforcement (Tripod Structure)

Nigeria's pluralistic system rests on three pillars: English-derived Common Law (universal), Customary Law (ethnic), Sharia (Islamic in North). English official in courts. No major 2025 reforms; MURIC pushes for southern Sharia courts for Muslims.

1. Common Law

Based on English precedents; applies nationwide for criminal (Criminal Code South, Penal Code North), civil, contracts, torts, federal matters. Supreme Court binds all. 2025: Enhanced digital filing in courts.

  • Criminal: Murder (death penalty), theft (imprisonment).
  • Civil: Contracts enforceable; arbitration encouraged (Arbitration Act 2023).

2. Sharia Law

In 12 northern states (e.g., Kano, Zamfara) since 1999; personal (marriage, inheritance) and criminal for consenting Muslims (Penal Code). Applies to all in Sharia areas for some offenses (e.g., alcohol ban). Non-Muslims opt out for personal matters. 2025: Advocacy for voluntary southern Sharia courts.

  • Hudud: Theft (amputation, rare), adultery (stoning, not enforced).
  • Sharia Courts of Appeal handle appeals.

3. Customary Law

Unwritten ethnic traditions (e.g., Yoruba, Igbo); valid if not repugnant to natural justice/equity/public policy. Used in Area/Customary Courts for family, land, chieftaincy. Coexists with common law.

  • Examples: Bride price in marriages, communal land tenure.
  • Appeals to High Court.

Key Enforcement Agencies (2025)

NPF (Police): General law/order; 0800-CALL-POLICE. Reforms: Community policing enhanced.
EFCC: Anti-corruption/fraud; 419 scams. 2025: Digital tracking improved.
NDLEA: Drugs; airport vigilance. Zero tolerance; life sentences.
DSS: Internal security; no public contact.
ICPC: Bribery; report public corruption.
NDPA 2023: Data privacy; fines up to 2% turnover for breaches.

Tip: In disputes, seek embassy/legal aid; avoid bribes (criminal under ICPC).

Visa & Immigration (NIS) - Comprehensive Guide 2025

Governed by Immigration Act 2015 & Visa Policy 2020 (updated 2025). All via portal.immigration.gov.ng. Key 2025 changes: VoA phased out May 1; e-Visa mandatory; Landing/Exit Cards required May 1; overstay penalties strict Aug 1 ($50-100/day + deportation/bans). Prohibited: Dual/multiple applications; work on non-work visas (deportation).

Application Process (e-Visa Mandatory 2025)

  1. Online Portal: Register at portal.immigration.gov.ng; upload passport scan, photo, docs.
  2. Pay Fee: Via card/Remita ($100-200); non-refundable.
  3. Approval Letter: 48-72 hours; print for travel (VoA replacement).
  4. At Airport: Present letter, passport; get visa stamp. Biometrics for first entry.
  5. Extensions: Apply 7 days before expiry at NIS office; $50 fee.

ECOWAS: Visa-free 90 days (tourism/business); present ID at entry. No work.

CERPAC Details

For >56 days/work; biometric card + ECI ($1,000/year insurance). Apply within 90 days of STR entry.

2025 Policy Changes

  • VoA End: May 1, 2025; e-Visa/Approval Letter required pre-flight.
  • Landing/Exit Cards: Mandatory May 1; complete online/at airport for tracking.
  • Overstay: Aug 1, 2025: $50/day (0-30 days), $100/day (>30); + deportation, 5-year ban.
  • Expat Quota: Stricter reviews; max 5% workforce foreigners.

How to Obtain a Driver's License (FRSC)

Foreigners resident in Nigeria must obtain a Nigerian Driver's License. International Driving Permits (IDP) are valid for one year only.

  1. 1

    Apply Online

    Visit nigeriadriverslicence.org. Click "DL Application".

  2. 2

    Pay Fees

    Pay via Remita platform. Cost is approx ‚Ç¶10,000 - ‚Ç¶15,000 (3-5 years).

  3. 3

    VIO Test & Biometrics

    Visit the Vehicle Inspection Office (VIO) for a driving test. Then proceed to FRSC center for biometric capture (photo/fingerprint).

  4. 4

    Temporary & Permanent

    You get a temporary printout valid for 60 days. The permanent card must be picked up personally.

Transport Safety Tips

  • Uber/Bolt: Recommended in Lagos, Abuja, and Port Harcourt. They are tracked and generally safe.
  • Inter-State: Use established bus lines (GUO, GIGM, ABC Transport) with private terminals. Avoid night travel due to poor visibility and security risks.
  • Air Travel: The safest way to travel between regions. Major carriers: Air Peace, Ibom Air, United Nigeria.
