A Visitor's Guide to the Constitution & Key Laws

Welcome from the NOA

On behalf of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, the National Orientation Agency (NOA) welcomes you. This guide will help you understand the 1999 Constitution, our **supreme law**, which all other laws must follow.

As a visitor, this document is important because it establishes our government's structure and grants **fundamental rights to *all persons* within Nigeria**, not just citizens. This guide also covers key laws on immigration, business, and conduct that you must follow during your stay.

A Federal Republic: Sharing Power

Nigeria is a **federal republic**, which means power is shared between different levels of government. You are subject to the laws of both the national (Federal) government and the specific state you are in.

1. The Federal Government

Headed by the President, this level handles national issues like defense, currency, immigration, and foreign affairs. It is based in the capital city, Abuja.

2. State Governments (36)

Each of Nigeria's 36 states has its own Governor, State Assembly, and Judiciary. They manage regional matters like local transportation, schools, and health services.

3. Federal Capital Territory (FCT)

The capital city of Abuja is not a state. It is special territory administered directly by the Federal Government through a Minister.

Separation of Powers

At the Federal and State levels, power is further divided into three branches to create a system of checks and balances:

  • The Executive: (The President / Governor) - Implements laws.
  • The Legislature: (National / State Assembly) - Makes laws.
  • The Judiciary: (The Courts) - Interprets laws.

Your Rights as a Person in Nigeria

Chapter IV of the Constitution grants these rights to **all persons**, regardless of nationality. While you are in Nigeria, you are protected.

Right to Life

You cannot be intentionally deprived of your life, except by a court sentence for a criminal offense.

Right to Dignity

You are protected from torture, slavery, forced labor, and any inhuman or degrading treatment.

Right to Personal Liberty

You cannot be arrested or detained unlawfully. If you are, you have the right to be told the reason and to be brought before a court quickly.

Right to a Fair Hearing

If you have a civil or criminal case, you are entitled to a fair and public trial by an impartial court. You are presumed innocent until proven guilty.

Freedom of Thought, Conscience & Religion

You are free to practice your religion (or no religion) and to hold your own beliefs without government interference.

Important: Limitations on Your Rights

These fundamental rights are not absolute. The Constitution allows them to be limited by law for specific, valid reasons, such as:

  • National Security
  • Public Health (e.g., quarantine or mandatory vaccination)
  • Public Order (e.g., curfews during unrest)
  • Protecting the rights and freedoms of other people.

Visitor Status: Rights vs. Privileges

While you are protected by fundamental *human* rights, the Constitution reserves certain *political* and *economic* rights exclusively for Nigerian citizens. Your ability to be in the country is a *privilege* governed by specific laws.

As a non-citizen, you:

  • Cannot vote in any Nigerian election.
  • Cannot run for political office (e.g., President, Governor, Senator).
  • Are not entitled to a Nigerian passport.
  • Do not have an automatic right to live or work in Nigeria without authorization.

Key Laws & Regulations for Visitors

1. Immigration & Residence

Governing Law: Immigration Act, 2015

  • Your entry and stay are dictated by your visa (Tourist, Business, Temporary Work Permit, etc.).
  • You cannot work on a Tourist or Business visa. Employment requires a specific visa (Subject to Regularization or STR) and a work permit.
  • For long-term residence (over 90 days), you must obtain a CERPAC (Combined Expatriate Residence Permit and Aliens Card), which is your "Green Card."

2. Work & Business

Governing Laws: NIPC Act & CAMA 2020

  • To start a business, you must register your company with the Corporate Affairs Commission (CAC).
  • Foreign-owned companies must register with the Nigerian Investment Promotion Commission (NIPC).
  • To hire foreign employees (including yourself), the company must obtain an Expatriate Quota from the Ministry of Interior.

3. Finance & Currency

Governing Body: Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN)

  • You must declare any foreign currency in cash or travelers' checks exceeding USD $10,000 (or its equivalent) upon entry or exit.
  • All foreign currency exchanges must be done through authorized dealers (i.e., banks). Using the "black market" for forex is illegal and risky.

4. Property & Land

Governing Law: Land Use Act, 1978

  • All land in Nigeria is technically owned by the State Governor on behalf of the people.
  • Foreigners cannot own land outright.
  • You *can* acquire land through a long-term lease (e.g., 99 years), but this requires the official consent of the State Governor.

A Diverse Legal Landscape

Nigeria's history has created a unique and complex legal system. The type of law that applies can depend on the state you're in and the nature of the issue.

1. Common Law (The National Standard)

Inherited from the British, this is the foundation of Nigeria's legal system. It applies in all federal courts and is the primary system for criminal and civil matters in the Southern and Central states. It is codified as the Criminal Code in the South.

2. Sharia Law (Penal Code)

Several states in Northern Nigeria (currently 12) operate a Sharia legal system, which runs alongside Common Law. This is codified as the Penal Code, which incorporates Sharia principles for criminal matters. While it primarily applies to Muslims, its presence heavily influences local laws and customs (e.g., regarding alcohol, dress codes) that apply to everyone in those states.

3. Customary Law

This unwritten law is based on the age-old traditions and customs of Nigeria's 370+ ethnic groups. It is typically used in local communities to settle matters like land inheritance or family disputes, and it varies greatly from one community to another.

Responsibilities & Prohibitions

The Constitution establishes Nigeria as a nation governed by the **rule of law**. This applies to everyone within its bordersâ€”citizens and visitors alike.

As a guest in Nigeria, you are expected to:

  • Respect and obey all laws, whether they are federal, state, or local bylaws.
  • Respect the national symbols, such as the flag and national anthem.
  • Assist law enforcement agencies in their duties (e.D., providing information if you witness a crime).
  • Declare your income honestly and pay any taxes you may be liable for if you are working in Nigeria.

Specific Prohibitions & Conduct

Warning: Areas of Zero Tolerance

Violating the following laws can lead to severe penalties, including imprisonment and deportation.

  • Illegal Drugs: Nigeria has zero tolerance for drug trafficking, possession, or use. The NDLEA (National Drug Law Enforcement Agency) enforces this strictly.
  • Currency Black Market: Exchanging currency on the street is illegal. Use only banks or licensed "Bureau de Change" (BDC) operators.
  • Photography of Secure Sites: Do NOT photograph military installations, government buildings, police stations, airports, or armed personnel. This can lead to arrest.
  • Disrespect to Authority: Always be polite to police, immigration, military, and other officials. Publicly arguing with or insulting an officer can be considered a criminal offense.
  • Cultural & Religious Insensitivity: Be aware of local customs. In many areas (especially the North), modest dress is required. Public displays of affection may be frowned upon.
  • Illegal Exports: You cannot export Nigerian antiquities or artifacts without a permit from the National Commission for Museums and Monuments.
