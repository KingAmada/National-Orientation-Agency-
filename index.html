A Visitor's Guide to the Constitution & Key Laws

Welcome from the NOA

On behalf of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, the National Orientation Agency (NOA) welcomes you. This guide will help you understand the 1999 Constitution, our **supreme law**, which all other laws must follow.

As a visitor, this document is important because it establishes our government's structure and grants **fundamental rights to *all persons* within Nigeria**, not just citizens. This guide also covers key laws on immigration, business, and conduct that you must follow during your stay.

Government & Constitution

Nigeria is a **Federal Republic** with three tiers of government (Federal, State, and Local) and a strict **separation of powers** at the Federal and State levels.

1. The Federal Government

Based in Abuja, it handles national "Exclusive List" items: defense, currency, immigration, customs, and foreign affairs.

  • The Executive (The Presidency): Headed by the President, who is Head of State and Commander-in-Chief. The President implements laws and appoints ministers.
  • The Legislature (National Assembly): A bicameral body that makes laws.
    • The Senate: 109 members (3 from each state, 1 from FCT).
    • The House of Representatives: 360 members based on population.
  • The Judiciary (Federal Courts): Interprets laws. Includes the Supreme Court, Court of Appeal, and Federal High Court.

2. State Governments (36 States + FCT)

Each state, headed by a Governor, has its own executive, legislature (House of Assembly), and judiciary (State High Court, Magistrate Courts). They handle "Residual List" items like local transport, health, and primary education.

3. Local Government Areas (774 LGAs)

Headed by a Chairman, these bodies manage local markets, sanitation, and community matters.

Your Fundamental Rights (Chapter IV)

Chapter IV of the Constitution grants these rights to **all persons**, regardless of nationality.

Right to Life: You cannot be intentionally deprived of your life, except by a court sentence.

Right to Dignity: You are protected from torture, slavery, and inhuman or degrading treatment.

Right to Personal Liberty: You cannot be arrested or detained unlawfully. If you are, you must be told the reason and brought before a court quickly.

Right to a Fair Hearing: You are entitled to a fair, public trial by an impartial court and are presumed innocent until proven guilty.

Right to Private & Family Life: Your privacy, home, and correspondence are protected from arbitrary interference.

Freedom of Thought, Conscience & Religion: You are free to practice your religion (or no religion).

Freedom of Expression: You have the right to hold and express opinions (this does not protect hate speech, defamation, or sedition).

Freedom of Assembly: You can assemble freely and associate with others, but this right is subject to public order.

Freedom of Movement: You have the right to move freely throughout Nigeria and reside in any part, *subject to your visa conditions*. This right does not override immigration law.

How to Enforce Your Rights

If you believe your fundamental rights have been breached, you have the right to seek redress.

  • You can apply to a State High Court or Federal High Court for enforcement.
  • You are entitled to legal representation. If you cannot afford one, contact the Legal Aid Council of Nigeria or your embassy/consulate.

Immigration & Residence

Your entry, stay, and work are governed by the Immigration Act, 2015, and managed by the Nigeria Immigration Service (NIS). Your visa is not a right; it is a privilege.

1. Key Visa Types

  • Visa on Arrival (VoA): Available to citizens of many countries (especially African Union members) and business visitors with pre-approval.
  • Business Visa: For meetings, conferences, or trade missions. You cannot work on this visa.
  • Temporary Work Permit (TWP): For short-term, specialized jobs (e.g., machinery installation, training). Usually valid for 3 months.
  • Subject to Regularization (STR) Visa: This is the long-term work visa. It's required for foreigners taking up full-time employment. This visa is the prerequisite for a CERPAC.

2. Expatriate Quota

A Nigerian company cannot hire a foreigner unless it has an "Expatriate Quota" approval from the Ministry of Interior. This is the company's responsibility, but the STR visa depends on it.

3. CERPAC (The "Green Card")

The Combined Expatriate Residence Permit and Aliens Card (CERPAC) is your official residence permit.

  • It is mandatory for any foreigner staying in Nigeria for more than 90 days (except diplomats and some others).
  • You must apply for it *after* arriving on an STR visa.
  • It is typically valid for 1-2 years and is renewable.

Doing Business & Investing

Nigeria actively encourages foreign investment, which is governed by several key laws.

1. Company Registration (CAMA 2020)

To do business, you must register your company with the Corporate Affairs Commission (CAC). Under the Companies and Allied Matters Act (CAMA) 2020, foreigners can fully own a Nigerian company.

2. Investor Protections (NIPC Act)

The Nigerian Investment Promotion Commission (NIPC) Act is your most important protection. It guarantees:

  • Repatriation of Profits: You can freely transfer your profits, dividends, and (after-tax) proceeds from a sale out of Nigeria in foreign currency, via an authorized bank.
  • Protection from Expropriation: The government cannot seize your business or property, except for national interest and with prompt, fair compensation.

3. Taxation (PITA & CITA)

Personal Income Tax Act (PITA): If you are resident in Nigeria for 183 days or more in any 12-month period, your worldwide income may be subject to Nigerian tax. Companies Income Tax Act (CITA): Registered companies pay tax on their profits.

4. Land (Land Use Act, 1978)

This is critical: Foreigners cannot own land outright in Nigeria. All land is vested in the State Governor. You can, however, acquire land via a long-term lease (e.g., 99 years), which requires the Governor's official consent.

5. Local Content (NOGICD Act 2010)

If your business is in the Oil & Gas sector, you MUST comply with the Nigerian Oil and Gas Industry Content Development (NOGICD) Act. This law mandates giving "first consideration" to Nigerian companies and labor for contracts and services.

Legal System & Law Enforcement

Nigeria operates a complex, multi-layered legal system.

1. Common Law: Inherited from Britain, this is the basis for most criminal and civil law, especially in Southern and Central states (the Criminal Code).

2. Sharia Law: In 12 Northern states, the Penal Code incorporates Sharia law for criminal matters. While it primarily applies to Muslims, it influences local customs (e.g., on alcohol) that affect everyone.

3. Customary Law: Unwritten, ethnic-based traditions used to settle local disputes (e.g., inheritance, family matters).

Law Enforcement Agencies to Know

Nigeria Police Force (NPF): Responsible for general law and order, crime prevention, and traffic control. They are your first point of contact.

Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC): A powerful agency targeting financial fraud ("419"), money laundering, and cybercrime.

Independent Corrupt Practices Commission (ICPC): Focuses on bribery and corruption involving public officials.

National Drug Law Enforcement Agency (NDLEA): Manages all drug-related offenses. Penalties are severe.

Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA) 2023: If your business handles Nigerian citizens' data, you must comply with this modern privacy law, which is similar to GDPR.

Health, Safety & Culture

1. Emergency & Health

Emergency Numbers: The national emergency numbers are 112 and 767. Save them.

Embassy/Consulate: Always have the 24/7 emergency contact number for your country's embassy or consulate. They are your primary support in a serious crisis.

Health Risks: Malaria is endemic. You must take prophylaxis and use mosquito nets/repellent. Drink only bottled or boiled water. Be cautious with street food.

Hospitals: Public hospitals are often under-resourced. For serious matters, it is highly recommended to use private clinics, which are common in major cities like Lagos and Abuja. Ensure you have comprehensive travel health insurance.

2. Cultural Etiquette

Greetings: Greetings are essential. Always ask "How are you?" or "Good morning/afternoon" before starting a conversation. Rushing is seen as rude.

Respect for Elders: Hierarchy is important. Greet elders first. In Yorubaland, a slight bow (for men) or curtsy (for women) is a sign of respect.

Right Hand: Always use your right hand to give or receive items, especially money or food. The left hand is considered unclean.

"Nigerian Time": Punctuality is important for formal business meetings, but social events or general appointments may run on "Nigerian time," which is more relaxed.

Verify or research the national emergency numbers (112 and 767) for accuracy. Double-check health recommendations (malaria, water). Ensure cultural etiquette points are accurate and respectfully phrased.

Key Prohibitions (Warning)

Violating the following laws can lead to severe penalties, including imprisonment and deportation. Ignorance of the law is not an excuse.

Areas of Zero Tolerance

  • Illegal Drugs: Nigeria has zero tolerance for drug trafficking, possession, or use, enforced by the NDLEA. Penalties include life imprisonment.
  • Currency Black Market: Exchanging currency on the street ("black market") is illegal. Use only banks or licensed "Bureau de Change" (BDC) operators.
  • Photography of Secure Sites: Do NOT photograph military installations, government buildings, police stations, airports, bridges, or armed personnel. This can lead to arrest and confiscation of your equipment.
  • Disrespect to Authority: Always be polite to police, immigration, military, and other officials. Publicly arguing with, insulting, or *especially* assaulting an officer is a serious criminal offense.
  • Cybercrimes (Cybercrimes Act 2015): Nigeria has strict laws against online fraud ("Yahoo Yahoo"), phishing, and identity theft. Do not engage in any activity that could be perceived as such.
  • Illegal Exports: You cannot export Nigerian antiquities, artifacts, or certain animal products without a permit from the National Commission for Museums and Monuments.
  • Cultural & Religious Insensitivity: Be aware of local customs. In many areas (especially the North), modest dress is required. Public displays of affection are often frowned upon. Blasphemy is a serious offense.
